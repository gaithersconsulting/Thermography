Our Services

A thermographer uses thermal imaging to detect various types of problems with roofs and in electrical systems that might not be visible to the naked eye, which helps in identifying potential problems before they lead to failures or hazards. These problems often involve issues related to moisture, insulation, and structural integrity. Here are some common problems a thermographer might find:

Moisture Intrusion

Water Leaks: Thermography can detect areas where water has infiltrated the roofing materials, which may not yet be visible as leaks inside the building. The presence of moisture often shows up as cooler spots on the thermal image because water retains heat differently than dry materials.

Trapped Moisture: In flat or low-sloped roofs, water can become trapped between layers, leading to insulation damage and potential structural problems. Thermography can identify these areas before they become severe.

Overheating Components

Loose Connections: Loose or corroded electrical connections can create high resistance, leading to localized heating. These areas appear as hot spots in thermal images, indicating a potential failure point.

Overloaded Circuits: Circuits carrying more current than they are designed for can overheat. Thermography can detect these hot circuits, allowing for corrective action before a potential failure or fire.

Heat Loss: In cold climates, heat loss through the roof can significantly impact a building's energy efficiency. A thermographer can pinpoint areas where heat is escaping, which can indicate poor insulation, gaps, or other issues.

Insulation Deficiencies

Missing or Damaged Insulation: Poor or missing insulation can cause heat loss in cold climates or excessive heat gain in hot climates, leading to higher energy costs. A thermographer can identify uneven temperature patterns that suggest insulation issues.

Thermal Bridging: Thermal bridging occurs when heat transfers through conductive materials like metal fasteners, reducing the overall effectiveness of insulation. These bridges create hot or cold spots that are easily visible in thermal imaging.

Roof Aging and Wear

Material Degradation: Over time, roofing materials degrade due to UV exposure, weather conditions, and other environmental factors. Thermography can help identify areas where the roofing material is beginning to fail, showing up as irregular thermal patterns.

Debonding of Roofing Layers: This can happen in multi-layer roofing systems, leading to reduced protection against the elements. Thermography can detect these issues early, allowing for timely repairs.

Thermography is a powerful tool for identifying a range of potential problems with roofs, from moisture intrusion to insulation deficiencies and structural issues. By detecting these problems early, building owners and managers can take proactive steps to maintain the integrity of the roof, enhance energy efficiency, and prevent costly repairs down the line.

Structural Issues

Delamination: Roofing materials, especially in older buildings, can start to delaminate (separate into layers), which weakens the roof's integrity. Thermography can detect differences in thermal patterns that indicate delamination.

Blisters and Voids: These are common in flat roofs with built-up membranes. Blisters and voids can lead to leaks and further degradation of the roof structure. They appear as unusual thermal patterns, often warmer or cooler than surrounding areas.

Ventilation Issues

Blocked Vents: Poor ventilation can lead to heat build-up, which can be detrimental to the roofing materials and the overall structure. Thermal imaging can identify areas with insufficient airflow, often revealed as hot spots.

Condensation Problems: Poor ventilation can also lead to condensation build-up inside the roof structure, which can cause moisture problems and mold growth. Thermography can help detect these issues by identifying temperature anomalies associated with condensation.

Imbalanced Loads

Uneven Load Distribution: In three-phase electrical systems, an imbalance in load distribution across phases can cause one phase to carry more current, leading to overheating. A thermographer can identify this imbalance by comparing the thermal signatures of each phase.

Harmonic Distortion: Non-linear loads can cause harmonic distortion, leading to overheating in certain components. Thermography can help detect these issues by revealing unusual heating patterns.

Electrical Arcing

Arcing in Switchgear or Panels: Electrical arcing occurs when there is a discharge of electricity across an air gap. This can lead to equipment damage or even fires. Thermography can detect the excessive heat generated by arcing before it causes significant damage.

Corroded or Damaged Contacts: Corrosion or damage to contacts can lead to arcing and overheating. A thermographer can detect these issues by identifying areas with abnormal temperature rises.

Thermography is an essential tool for detecting a wide range of problems in electrical systems, including overheating components, imbalanced loads, insulation failures, and more. By identifying these issues early, maintenance teams can take corrective actions to prevent equipment failures, reduce downtime, and enhance safety.

Overheating Motors and Bearings

Motor Overload: Motors that are overloaded or operating under mechanical stress will generate excess heat. A thermographer can identify motors that are running hotter than normal, indicating a potential problem.

Bearing Failure: Bearings in motors and other rotating equipment can overheat due to lubrication issues, misalignment, or wear. Thermal imaging can reveal these overheating bearings before they fail, preventing costly downtime.

Transformer Issues

Overheating Coils: Transformers can develop hot spots due to coil winding issues or insulation breakdown. Thermography can detect these hot spots, indicating potential transformer problems.

Cooling System Failures: Transformers rely on cooling systems to maintain safe operating temperatures. If a cooling system fails, the transformer may overheat. Thermography can help detect areas where the cooling system is not functioning correctly.

Switchgear and Panelboard Problems

Hot Spots in Switchgear: Switchgear, which controls, protects, and isolates electrical equipment, can develop hot spots due to issues like loose connections, component degradation, or overloading. Thermal imaging can help identify these problems.

Panelboard Overheating: Overloaded or faulty panelboards can overheat, leading to potential electrical fires. A thermographer can detect overheating components within the panelboard, allowing for timely maintenance.

Poorly Ventilated Electrical Enclosures

Heat Build-up: Electrical enclosures that are poorly ventilated can accumulate heat, leading to the overheating of components inside. Thermography can detect excessive heat within enclosures, suggesting the need for better ventilation or cooling.

Thermal Runaway: In extreme cases, poor ventilation can lead to thermal runaway, where the heat generated within the enclosure increases uncontrollably, leading to equipment failure or fire. Early detection with thermal imaging can prevent this.
